Malaria in Liberia: Prevention, Symptoms and Treatment

June 5, 2025· Ducor International Pharmacy· 6 min read

Malaria kills thousands of Liberians every year and affects millions more. It is the single biggest infectious disease threat in the country. But the good news is that malaria is preventable, and when caught early, it is treatable. What matters most is acting fast.

What Is Malaria?

Malaria is a disease caused by parasites called Plasmodium, transmitted through the bite of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. These mosquitoes typically bite between dusk and dawn. In Liberia, the most dangerous type — Plasmodium falciparum — is responsible for most cases and can become life-threatening within 24 hours if untreated.

Know the Symptoms

Malaria symptoms usually appear 7 to 14 days after a mosquito bite. The first signs are easy to mistake for flu:

  • High fever — often sudden and severe, may come in cycles
  • Chills and shaking — feeling cold even when the temperature is high
  • Severe headache
  • Muscle and body aches
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Nausea, vomiting, or diarrhoea
  • Sweating heavily after the fever breaks
Go to hospital or pharmacy immediately if you or a child has high fever with chills. In children, malaria can cause convulsions and become fatal within hours. Do not wait and see.

Severe Malaria — Danger Signs

These symptoms mean the malaria is severe and is a medical emergency:

  • Confusion, difficulty waking, or unconsciousness
  • Convulsions (seizures)
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Yellow eyes or skin (jaundice)
  • Very dark or brown urine ("blackwater fever")
  • Inability to keep fluids down
Severe malaria is a life-threatening emergency. Go to the nearest hospital immediately. Do not wait for the pharmacy.

How to Prevent Malaria

Prevention is far better than treatment. Here is how to protect your family:

Sleep Under a Treated Mosquito Net

Long-lasting insecticidal nets (LLINs) are the single most effective way to prevent malaria. They are especially important for children under 5 and pregnant women. Make sure the net has no holes and is tucked under the mattress properly.

Use Mosquito Repellent

Apply repellent containing DEET or picaridin on exposed skin, especially in the evenings. Burning mosquito coils or using plug-in repellents indoors also helps.

Eliminate Standing Water

Mosquitoes breed in standing water. Empty buckets, flowerpots, tyres, and containers around your home. Keep water storage containers tightly covered. Clear gutters of debris so water drains freely.

Wear Protective Clothing

In the evenings, wear long sleeves and long trousers to reduce skin exposure to mosquitoes.

Preventive Medicine for High-Risk Groups

Pregnant women in Liberia should receive Intermittent Preventive Treatment (IPTp) during antenatal visits. Children under 5 in high transmission areas may benefit from Seasonal Malaria Chemoprevention — speak to our pharmacist for guidance.

Treatment

If you suspect malaria, do not self-treat with leftover medicines — the parasite may be resistant to what you have. The treatment depends on the type of malaria and its severity.

The WHO-recommended first-line treatment for uncomplicated falciparum malaria in Liberia is Artemisinin-based Combination Therapy (ACT). This requires a prescription. Complete the full course even if you feel better — stopping early allows resistant parasites to survive.

Complete your full course. Most people feel much better within 24–48 hours of starting treatment, but the parasites are still present. Stopping early is dangerous and leads to resistance.
